Title: Opening a Bank Account in the Bahamas
Instructor: Finsight Lite Team
Grade Level: 7-12
Subject: Financial Literacy
Duration: 15 minutes
Topic: Banking Basics

Learning Objectives:
- Understand the difference between a chequing account and a savings account
- Know what documents and information are needed to open an account in the Bahamas
- Understand why a parent or guardian is usually required for anyone under 18
- Recognize the ongoing costs (fees, minimum balances) that come with having an
  account

Content Section 1 Heading: Chequing vs. Savings, What's the Difference?
Content Section 1 Paragraph 1: A chequing account (sometimes called a current
account) is built for everyday spending. Money moves in and out often, you might
get a debit card with it, and some chequing accounts let you write cheques or pay
bills directly.
Content Section 1 Paragraph 2: A savings account is built to hold money you're
not touching often. It usually earns a small amount of interest, which is the
bank paying you for keeping your money there, but many savings accounts limit how
many withdrawals you can make each month before a fee kicks in. Most banks in the
Bahamas, including Scotiabank, RBC, CIBC FirstCaribbean, Fidelity Bank, and Bank
of The Bahamas, offer both. A common approach: use a chequing account for
spending money and a savings account for money you're setting aside for a goal.
[diagramKey: chequing-vs-savings applies after this section]

Content Section 2 Heading: What You Actually Need to Open One
Content Section 2 Paragraph 1: Since most students on Finsight Lite are under 18,
banks in the Bahamas typically require a parent or guardian to open the account
with you, either as a joint account holder or as a co-signer.
Content Section 2 Paragraph 2: You'll usually need a valid form of ID (a school
ID, birth certificate, or passport), proof of address (a utility bill in a
parent's name works), and a parent or guardian present with their own ID. Some
banks also ask for an initial deposit to open the account, which varies by bank
and account type, so it's worth calling ahead or checking the bank's website
before showing up.
[diagramKey: bank-account-steps applies after this section]

Content Section 3 Heading: The Costs Nobody Mentions Upfront
Content Section 3 Paragraph 1: Having a bank account isn't free. Watch for
monthly maintenance fees (charged just for having the account open), minimum
balance requirements (some accounts charge a fee if your balance drops below a
set amount), and ATM fees (using an ATM outside your bank's network usually costs
extra).
Content Section 3 Paragraph 2: None of this means you shouldn't open an account,
a bank account is a foundational money tool, it just means reading the fee
schedule before you sign up saves you from surprises later.

PART 4: BAHAMAS LESSON 2, "Understanding Your Payslip and NIB"
============================================================

Title: Understanding Your Payslip and NIB
Instructor: Finsight Lite Team
Grade Level: 9-12
Subject: Financial Literacy
Duration: 15 minutes
Topic: Payroll and Government Deductions

Learning Objectives:
- Read and understand the basic parts of a Bahamian payslip
- Understand what NIB (National Insurance Board) is and why contributions are
  deducted
- Know what NIB contributions eventually provide
- Understand the difference between gross pay and net pay

Content Section 1 Heading: Gross Pay vs. Net Pay
Content Section 1 Paragraph 1: Gross pay is the full amount you earned before
anything is taken out. Net pay, sometimes called take-home pay, is what actually
lands in your account after deductions.
Content Section 1 Paragraph 2: The biggest deduction most working people in the
Bahamas will see on a payslip is NIB. If you've never seen a payslip before, the
gap between gross and net pay can be surprising, understanding it now means it
won't catch you off guard at your first job.
[diagramKey: payslip-breakdown applies after this section]

Content Section 2 Heading: What Is NIB?
Content Section 2 Paragraph 1: NIB, the National Insurance Board, is the
Bahamas' national social security programme. Almost every employed person in the
Bahamas contributes to NIB, and it's automatically deducted from your paycheck,
similar to how other countries have their own national insurance or social
security systems.
Content Section 2 Paragraph 2: NIB contributions fund benefits that support
people through major life events: sickness benefit, maternity benefit,
retirement pension, and support for family members if a worker passes away or is
injured on the job. It's not a savings account you can withdraw from anytime,
it's a shared national system that pays out when specific, defined situations
happen.

Content Section 3 Heading: Why This Matters Even Before Your First Job
Content Section 3 Paragraph 1: Understanding NIB before you're actually holding a
payslip means you won't be confused or worried the first time you see a chunk of
your pay missing.
Content Section 3 Paragraph 2: It also matters long-term: NIB contributions are
what eventually qualify someone for a retirement pension, so a consistent work
history with NIB contributions isn't just a deduction today, it's building toward
something later.

A) There is no difference, they mean the same thing

D) Net pay is always higher than gross pay
Correct: B

2. What does NIB stand for?
A) National Investment Bank
B) National Insurance Board
C) Nassau Income Bureau
D) National Interest Balance
Correct: B

3. Which of these is something NIB contributions can eventually provide?
A) A free car
B) A retirement pension
C) Free tuition at any school
D) A guaranteed loan
Correct: B

4. Why is NIB deducted automatically from a paycheck?
A) It's optional but most people forget to opt out
B) It's a mandatory national social security contribution
C) It's a penalty for late tax filing
D) It only applies to business owners
Correct: B

PART 6: JAMAICA LESSON 2, "Understanding JAM-DEX, Jamaica's Digital Dollar"
============================================================

Territory-scoped: only visible to students in the Jamaica (JMD) environment.

Title: Understanding JAM-DEX, Jamaica's Digital Dollar
Instructor: Finsight Lite Team
Grade Level: 9-12
Subject: Financial Literacy
Duration: 15 minutes
Topic: Digital Currency in Jamaica

Learning Objectives:
- Understand what JAM-DEX is and how it differs from a regular bank account
- Know how to open and use a JAM-DEX wallet
- Understand why a new form of money doesn't automatically get used by everyone
  right away
- Recognize JAM-DEX as a real, legal-tender example of digital money, not a
  cryptocurrency

Content Section 1 Heading: What Is JAM-DEX?
Content Section 1 Paragraph 1: JAM-DEX is Jamaica's own digital dollar, created
and backed by the Bank of Jamaica (BOJ). It's worth exactly the same as a regular
Jamaican dollar, one JAM-DEX dollar equals one physical Jamaican dollar, and it's
legal tender, meaning it's an officially recognized form of money, not something
separate or unofficial. This makes Jamaica one of only a handful of countries in
the world with a fully launched central bank digital currency, often shortened to
CBDC.
Content Section 1 Paragraph 2: It's important not to confuse JAM-DEX with
cryptocurrency like Bitcoin. Cryptocurrency isn't issued or backed by any
government, its value can swing wildly, and no central bank stands behind it.
JAM-DEX is the opposite: it's issued directly by Jamaica's central bank, its
value never moves against the regular Jamaican dollar, and BOJ stands behind it
the same way it stands behind physical cash.

Content Section 2 Heading: How It Actually Works
Content Section 2 Paragraph 1: To use JAM-DEX, you need a digital wallet,
available through banks and wallet providers like NCB's Lynk and JN Bank's JN
Pay. A wallet can either be linked to an existing bank account, or opened
independently with basic information like name, address, and date of birth. Once
it's set up, you can add money by transferring from a bank account or depositing
cash at a participating branch or smart ATM.
Content Section 2 Paragraph 2: To pay someone, you scan a QR code or enter their
wallet ID and send the amount, similar to how many mobile payment apps work
elsewhere in the world, except this one is actual government-issued money rather
than a private company's payment system.

Content Section 3 Heading: Why Hasn't Everyone Adopted It?
Content Section 3 Paragraph 1: Here's the honest part of the story: even though
JAM-DEX launched back in 2021, most people in Jamaica still aren't using it day
to day, and it's worth understanding why, because it teaches something bigger
than just this one currency. As of 2026, JAM-DEX still makes up a very small
share of all the money moving through Jamaica's economy, even though the amount
being used has grown quickly.
Content Section 3 Paragraph 2: The core problem is what's sometimes called a
chicken-and-egg situation: many stores don't accept JAM-DEX yet because their
card machines haven't been upgraded to take it, and many people don't bother
using JAM-DEX because there aren't enough places to actually spend it. Each side
is waiting on the other.
Content Section 3 Paragraph 3: The Bank of Jamaica has publicly pushed banks to
move faster on upgrading their systems, even offering to cover part of the cost,
but the rollout has still taken longer than planners originally expected.
Content Section 3 Paragraph 4: This is a useful real-world lesson: a new form of
money can be well-designed and government-backed and still take years to catch
on, because it isn't just about the technology working, it's about enough people
and enough businesses deciding to actually use it at the same time.

Quiz Questions:
1. What backs the value of JAM-DEX?
A) Nothing, its value is determined entirely by trading
B) The Bank of Jamaica, and it's worth the same as a regular Jamaican dollar
C) A private technology company
D) Gold reserves held overseas
Correct: B

2. How is JAM-DEX different from a cryptocurrency like Bitcoin?
A) There is no real difference, they work the same way
B) JAM-DEX is issued and backed by Jamaica's central bank, cryptocurrency is not
C) Cryptocurrency is legal tender and JAM-DEX is not
D) JAM-DEX can only be used outside of Jamaica
Correct: B

3. How do you typically pay someone using JAM-DEX?
A) By mailing physical cash
B) By scanning a QR code or entering their wallet ID
C) Only in person at a Bank of Jamaica branch
D) It cannot be used for payments, only savings
Correct: B

4. What is the main reason JAM-DEX adoption has been slower than expected?
A) It is illegal to use in most of Jamaica
B) A chicken-and-egg problem, where merchants and customers are each waiting on
   the other
C) Nobody in Jamaica owns a smartphone
D) The Bank of Jamaica discontinued the program
Correct: B

5. What's a broader lesson JAM-DEX's slow adoption can teach about new forms of
money?
A) Government-backed currency always gets adopted instantly
B) A new form of money needs enough people and businesses using it together, not
   just good technology
C) Digital currency never works and should be avoided
D) Only cryptocurrencies face adoption challenges
Correct: B

PART 8: TRINIDAD LESSON 2, "Know Your Money, Trinidad and Tobago's Currency"
============================================================

Territory-scoped: only visible to students in the Trinidad and Tobago (TTD)
environment.

Title: Know Your Money, Trinidad and Tobago's Currency
Instructor: Finsight Lite Team
Grade Level: 7-12
Subject: Financial Literacy
Duration: 10 minutes
Topic: Currency Literacy and Security

Learning Objectives:
- Know how to check that a T&T banknote is genuine using the Feel, Look, Tilt,
  Check method
- Understand why recognizing counterfeit currency is a real, practical skill
- Recognize the Central Bank of Trinidad and Tobago's Know Your Money campaign as
  the source of this guidance
- Understand that Trinidad and Tobago has deliberately taken a cautious approach
  to digital currency, unlike some other Caribbean territories

Content Section 1 Heading: How to Check a Note Is Genuine
Content Section 1 Paragraph 1: Trinidad and Tobago's banknotes are made of
polymer, a plastic-like material, and the Central Bank runs a public education
campaign called Know Your Money, teaching a simple four-step method to check if a
note is genuine: Feel, Look, Tilt, and Check. The Central Bank is also rolling out
updated banknotes over the next year, which makes knowing these security features
even more useful right now.
Content Section 1 Paragraph 2: Feel: polymer notes are noticeably smoother than
paper, and genuine notes have raised, triangular dots in the bottom corner you
can feel with your finger. Look: hold the note up and look for a clear window
built into the polymer that you can see through from both the front and back.
Content Section 1 Paragraph 3: Tilt: certain printed elements shift or shimmer
when you tilt the note at an angle. Check: compare the note against the Central
Bank's own reference images if you're ever unsure.
Content Section 1 Paragraph 4: This isn't just useful trivia, knowing how to spot
a fake is a real, practical skill.

Content Section 2 Heading: Why T&T Hasn't Gone Digital (Yet)
Content Section 2 Paragraph 1: Some other Caribbean countries, including the
Bahamas and Jamaica, have launched their own digital currencies. Trinidad and
Tobago has not, and that's a deliberate choice rather than a delay. The Central
Bank of Trinidad and Tobago has studied the idea for years but has stated it will
not move forward with a digital currency for now, citing concerns about
financial stability and risk.
Content Section 2 Paragraph 2: Instead, T&T's Central Bank has focused its
public education efforts on programmes like Know Your Money, teaching people to
recognize genuine physical currency, and on modernizing the payment system in
other ways.
Content Section 2 Paragraph 3: This is worth understanding as its own kind of
decision: not every country needs to adopt the same technology at the same pace,
and being cautious about a major financial change is itself a legitimate
approach, not just a country falling behind.

PART 11: MODULES DEFAULT TO COLLAPSED ON THE LESSONS TAB
============================================================

On the student-facing Lessons tab (the Core Curriculum view showing module cards
like Budgeting Basics, Saving Smart, Investing Fundamentals, The Sand Dollar, and
the new Real Life Ready), each module card currently has an expand/collapse
chevron. Check the current default state when a student first opens the Lessons
tab.

If modules currently default to expanded (showing the full lesson list inside
immediately), change this so every module defaults to collapsed instead, showing
only the module header, icon, subtitle, progress bar, and percentage complete.
The student then taps the chevron or the card itself to expand it and see the
individual lessons inside. This reduces how overwhelming the Lessons tab feels
when a student first opens it, especially now that there are five modules instead
of four.

This applies to all modules, not just Real Life Ready, existing modules
(Budgeting Basics, Saving Smart, Investing Fundamentals, The Sand Dollar) should
also default to collapsed for consistency, a student shouldn't see one module
behave differently from another.

Requirements for this change:
- The collapse/expand interaction should already exist given the chevron is
  already present, this is a default-state change plus confirming the animation
  on expand/collapse feels intentional (a smooth height transition, not an
  abrupt snap), consistent with the animations-default-on standing rule
- If more than one module can be expanded at once currently, decide whether that
  should stay as is or whether opening one module should collapse others
  (accordion-style). Default to allowing multiple modules open at once unless the
  existing pattern elsewhere in the app clearly favors accordion-style
  single-open behavior, match whatever the existing pattern is rather than
  inventing a new one
- Verify this in both light and dark mode
- Verify the collapsed state still clearly shows enough information for a
  student to know what a module is and how far into it they are (icon, title,
  subtitle, progress bar, percentage) without needing to expand it first
